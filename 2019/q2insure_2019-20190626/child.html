Molina Healthcare reported lower revenue and net income in the second quarter, as Medicaid contract losses weighed on its financial results. Because the insurer lost a contract in New Mexico in 2018 and this year is serving fewer Medicaid regions in Florida, it enrolled fewer members and brought in less premium revenue. On the flip side, Molina reported lower medical costs and administrative expenses. The company’s after-tax margin was 4.7%. Molina CEO Joseph Zubretsky said the company’s performance shows it can “sustain an attractive margin profile while pivoting to top-line growth.”

Big membership growth in Humana’s Medicare Advantage plans helped propel the insurer’s top line in the second quarter of 2019. Humana grew individual Advantage membership 15.1% to 3.5 million—the highest growth the company has seen in a decade, CEO Bruce Broussard said. Still, Humana lost members in its Medicare stand-alone prescription drug plans and fully insured commercial employer plans. The company’s big year-over-year increase in net income occurred because Humana sold its long-term care subsidiary at a nearly $800 million loss in the second quarter of last year.

Even though Anthem reported an increase in revenue and net income in the second quarter of 2019, those results were overshadowed by continued high medical costs in its Medicaid business.
